Stock Metrics

Use these numbers to judge if a company's stock is worth buying — or overpriced hype.

Good to buy
Be careful
Danger zone
P/E Ratio
Price to Earnings
Most important
Imagine a samosa stall earns ₹10/day. If you pay ₹150 to own it, your P/E is 15. It tells you how many years of earnings you're paying for today.
Ideal Range
Under 25 — Good value 25–40 — Pricey but okay Above 50 — Overpriced
EPS
Earnings Per Share
Profit health
If a company made ₹1 crore profit and has 10 lakh shares, each share "earned" ₹10. That's the EPS. Higher & growing EPS = company getting richer every year.
What to look for
Growing every year — Great sign Flat — Okay, not exciting Falling — Big red flag
ROE
Return on Equity
Efficiency
You give a friend ₹100 to start a shop. If they return ₹20 profit, ROE is 20%. It tells you how good the company is at turning YOUR money into MORE money.
Ideal Range
Above 15% — Excellent 10–15% — Average Below 10% — Weak
Debt to Equity
D/E Ratio
Risk checker
Your friend's shop is worth ₹1 lakh but they borrowed ₹2 lakh to run it — very risky! D/E shows how much the company borrowed vs how much it actually owns.
Ideal Range
Under 0.5 — Very safe 0.5–1.5 — Manageable Above 2 — Danger
P/B Ratio
Price to Book
Value check
A company's total assets are worth ₹100 but you buy it for ₹80 — that's a bargain! P/B compares the stock price to what the company actually physically owns.
Ideal Range
Under 1 — Hidden gem 1–3 — Normal Above 5 — Expensive
Dividend Yield
Passive income %
Income
Companies share profits with you yearly — like rent from a flat you own. If you paid ₹1000 for stock and get ₹30/year, dividend yield is 3%. Free money while you hold.
What to look for
1–4% — Healthy & sustainable Above 6% — Could be a trap 0% — No passive income
Market Cap
Company size
Size matters
Total value of ALL shares combined — like the price tag on the whole company. Bigger = safer but slower growth. Smaller = fast growth potential but more risk.
In India (NSE/BSE)
Large Cap — Above ₹20,000 Cr Mid Cap — ₹5,000–20,000 Cr Small Cap — Below ₹5,000 Cr
52W High / Low
52-week price range
Price context
Highest & lowest price in the last 1 year. Like knowing a product was ₹500 last month — so ₹350 today is a deal. Helps you know if you're buying cheap or expensive.
Smart rule of thumb
Near 52W low — Possible bargain Near 52W high — Buying at peak
Golden rule for Indian stocks: Look for companies with P/E under 25, ROE above 15%, low D/E (under 1), and growing EPS year-on-year. Never invest based on a single number — use at least 3–4 together. Tools: Screener.in, Tickertape, Trendlyne — all free.

Mutual Fund Metrics

Mutual funds pool your money with thousands of others — a fund manager invests it. These help you pick the right one.

Good sign
Watch carefully
Avoid or reconsider
NAV
Net Asset Value
Unit price
NAV is the price of one "unit" of the fund. If NAV is ₹50 today and ₹70 after 2 years, you made profit. Lower NAV doesn't mean cheaper — it just means the fund is younger.
What matters
Rising NAV over years — Good Falling NAV — Losses
CAGR
Compounded Annual Growth Rate
Most important
You invest ₹10,000 and it grows to ₹20,000 in 5 years. CAGR tells you the consistent yearly growth rate that got you there — like the fund's annual report card.
Ideal (5–10 year CAGR)
Above 12% — Excellent 8–12% — Good Below 8% — Barely beats FD
Expense Ratio
Annual fund charges
Hidden cost
The fund house charges a yearly fee to manage your money. At 1.5%, they take ₹150/year per ₹10,000 invested — even if the fund performs badly. Always go as low as possible!
What's acceptable
Under 0.5% — Very low (index funds) 0.5–1% — Acceptable 1–1.5% — Okay for active funds Above 1.5% — Too high
Sharpe Ratio
Risk-adjusted return
Return quality
Two students scored 80 — one studied calmly, one pulled all-nighters in panic. Sharpe checks if the fund earns good returns WITHOUT taking crazy risks. Higher is always better.
Ideal Value
Above 1 — Good risk-adjusted return 0.5–1 — Average Below 0.5 — Too risky for the return
Alpha
Extra return vs benchmark
Fund manager skill
Nifty 50 grew 10% and your fund grew 13% — Alpha is +3%. It shows how much EXTRA the fund manager earned by being smart, beyond just following the market blindly.
What to look for
Positive Alpha — Fund beats market Zero — Just follows index Negative — Worse than market
Beta
Market sensitivity
Volatility
Market falls 10% and your fund falls 15% — Beta is 1.5. Very jumpy! Beta = 1 means it moves like the market. Under 1 means it's calmer. Better for nervous investors.
Ideal range
Under 1 — Less volatile, safer Around 1 — Tracks market Above 1.5 — Very bumpy ride
AUM
Assets Under Management
Fund size
Total money ALL investors have put into this one fund. Bigger AUM = more people trust it. But too large a fund can become slow to react to market opportunities.
In Indian context
₹1,000 Cr+ — Well established ₹100–999 Cr — Growing Under ₹100 Cr — Too small, risky
Standard Deviation
Return consistency
Consistency
Some funds go 30% one year and -10% the next — like a rollercoaster. Standard deviation measures how "jumpy" the returns are. Lower = smoother, more predictable growth.
Equity funds (India)
Under 12% — Stable 12–18% — Normal volatility Above 20% — Very volatile
Golden rule for mutual funds: For beginners, start with a Nifty 50 Index Fund via SIP — low expense ratio (under 0.2%), consistent CAGR, and zero stress. Check CAGR over 5–10 years, not just last year. Use Groww, Zerodha Coin, or MF Central to invest free.
